Pillar 1

Instructional Writing

Readers judge the quality of instructions based on how easily they can use them to perform their tasks (Ch. 27, p. 458). Every writing decision in this guide was made with that standard in mind.

❓

Answer the six reader questions (Ch. 27, p. 458)

Nearly all readers bring the same six questions to instructions: what will this help me do, what do I need to know beforehand, what materials do I need, what exactly do I do, and how do I fix something that goes wrong. This guide is structured to answer all six, with each section handling a distinct part of the reader's task.

📋

Use the active voice and imperative mood (Ch. 27, p. 465)

Active, imperative verbs give commands directly and leave no room for interpretation. Every direction in this guide follows that principle: "Flip your top card," "Compare the two cards," "Award the cards." The result is less time spent reading and more time spent doing.

1️⃣

One action per step, number in its own column (Ch. 27, p. 466)

Readers easily overlook a direction tucked in with another rather than given its own number. Putting step numbers in a separate column also keeps them easy to spot when a reader looks back after performing an action. Every step in this guide contains one action, and the step number sits in its own circle to the left of the content.

⚠️

Place warnings before the relevant action (Ch. 27, p. 464)

A warning discovered after performing a step is too late to help. The Joker warning appears before Step 1 and the insufficient-cards warning appears before the step where that problem arises. Both callouts name the issue and tell the reader what to do about it (Ch. 27, p. 464).

🖼️

Use graphics to show what words cannot (Ch. 27, p. 467)

Graphics can show where things are, how to perform steps, and what a result should look like — things words alone cannot convey as efficiently. The example card comparisons in the Play section show a completed round visually rather than describing it. Visuals are placed directly beside the steps they support so readers can coordinate both easily.

🗂️

Group steps under action-oriented headings (Ch. 27, p. 467)

Dividing instructions into chunks under headings that use participles rather than nouns makes procedures feel manageable and scannable. The four parts of this guide follow that structure: "Setting Up the Game," "Playing a Round," "Resolving a Tie," and "Ending the Game." Readers who only need one section can find it without reading the rest.

Pillar 2

Reader-Centered Writing

Good instructions have both usefulness goals and persuasive goals (Ch. 27, p. 459). Usefulness ensures readers can complete the task; persuasion ensures they want to read in the first place. Both shaped every decision in this guide.

Identify your audience (Ch. 27, p. 462)

Readers want to know whether instructions are directed at people like them. This guide targets beginners of all ages with no assumed prior knowledge. Game-specific terms are defined in context the first time they appear.

Write clearly and succinctly (Ch. 27, p. 460)

More words make more work for readers and raise the chance they stop reading altogether. Each step contains only the information needed to perform it, and supporting explanations are kept visually separate from the action itself (Ch. 27, p. 466).

Design for non-linear reading (Ch. 27, p. 459)

Instruction readers read a step, look away to do it, then return to find the next one. They do not read straight through. The sticky nav, anchor links, numbered steps, and progress bar all support this back-and-forth pattern, keeping readers oriented at every point.

Test with real users (Ch. 27, p. 460)

Nothing beats watching your target audience actually use a draft. This guide was reviewed by classmates on March 2, and that session directly led to moving the Card Ranks to the Overview and elevating the Joker warning to a prominent callout block.

Motivate readers to engage (Ch. 27, p. 463)

Many readers are tempted to skip instructions entirely. An inviting tone and attractive design can persuade them to engage. The hero section, overview grid, and interactive rank strip are designed to make this guide feel approachable before a reader has committed to a single step.

Present branching steps clearly (Ch. 27, p. 469)

When instructions branch, describe the decision point first, then format both alternatives clearly rather than listing only one. The Insufficient Cards section does exactly that: Scenario A and Scenario B are labeled distinctly so readers can identify which applies at a glance.

Pillar 3

Document and Website Design

Readers see the visual design of a page before they read its content, and what they see powerfully affects whether they find it useful and trustworthy (Ch. 14, p. 261). Every visual decision in this guide responds to that reality.

📐

Alignment signals organization (Ch. 14, p. 267)

Aligning related elements with one another lets readers see how information is organized at a glance. Step numbers, titles, and body text each occupy a defined column in this guide. Spanning section headings across the full content width also gives them added emphasis, as the textbook notes that extending items across columns is one way to highlight them (p. 268).

🔲

Grouping shows relationships (Ch. 14, p. 268)

Readers interpret the distance between items as a signal of how closely related they are. Less space signals connection; more space signals separation. Using less space below a heading than above it places the heading in closer visual proximity to the content it labels (p. 269), a principle applied consistently to every section and step group in this guide.

🔴

Contrast establishes hierarchy and focus (Ch. 14, p. 270)

Nothing stands out when everything looks the same. Size, type treatment, color, and font are the four variables that create contrast (p. 270). Red is used consistently here for section labels, active nav states, and warning callouts, making high-priority content immediately visible without readers having to search for it.

🔁

Repetition creates visual unity (Ch. 14, p. 272)

Repetition lets readers learn a document's structure so each new page feels immediately familiar. Every page on this site shares the same nav bar, footer, font pairing, color palette, and callout styles. A reader who understands the layout of the Overview page can orient themselves instantly on any other page without any extra effort.

🌐

Apply print design principles to web pages (Ch. 27, p. 476)

The same design strategies that make print documents usable make web pages usable too. A consistent design across all pages and the alignment, grouping, contrast, and repetition principles drawn from Chapter 14 together ensure this site is as intentionally designed as any printed manual.

♿

Select type that is easy to read (Ch. 14, p. 274)

Fonts with strong, distinct main lines are easier to read, and all-capital letters should be avoided for more than a few words since research shows they slow reading. This guide uses Playfair Display for headings and DM Sans for body text. Uppercase is reserved for short section labels only, not full sentences.
