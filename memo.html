Design Memo
Purpose & Definition of the Instructions

These instructions explain how to play War, a two-player card game of chance. They are designed to help any reader set up the game, play rounds confidently, resolve tie-breaking "War" scenarios, and determine a winner. Our goal was to answer the six questions readers most commonly bring to instructions: what will this help me do, what do I need to know beforehand, what materials are required, what exactly do I do, and how do I fix problems (Ch. 27, p. 458).

Audience Analysis & Document Design

Our primary audience is anyone interested in learning War for the first time. This includes, beginners of all ages, younger players, and casual adult players. Because this audience cannot be assumed to possess any prior card-game knowledge, we wrote in plain, imperative language ("Flip your top card," "Compare the two cards") as recommended in Chapter 27 (p. 465), using active voice and the imperative mood throughout. We avoided jargon and defined terms like "draw pile" and "battle card" for reader context. For document design, we followed the alignment, grouping, and contrast principles outlined in Chapter 14 (pp. 267–270). Related elements like step numbers, step titles, and body text, are aligned to the left within a clear grid, creating clear visual relationships (Ch. 14, p. 267). White space is used between steps so that readers can locate their place after looking away to perform an action (Ch. 27, p. 460). Warning and note callouts use contrast like red borders and a tinted background to make safety-relevant content stand out immediately (Ch. 14, p. 261). The sticky navigation bar with anchor links and the reading progress bar address the non-linear reading behavior that Ch. 27 (p. 459) identifies as how readers use instructions: they do not read straight through but can instead scan, jump, and return.

Usability Testing

On March 2, our team conducted a peer walkthrough originally for tacos. However, our circumstances unexpectedly changed with our prior access to a kitchen being made unavailable. From our taco instructions however, we learned we needed to include more visuals, clearer directions, and more whitespace between sections. We used that info when designing the website and instructions.

Collaboration & Co-Writing Narrative

Our team divided work into research & drafting, design, and testing. During research & drafting, we used a shared document to co-write the step-by-step directions, with each member responsible for one section (Setup, Playing a Round, War, and End Conditions). The design phase was led by one member but reviewed collectively using Ch. 14 grid and contrast principles. The most effective practice was reading each step aloud and asking "would someone new to this game know exactly what to do?" The least effective was asynchronous commenting on layout with visual design decisions needing in-person sessions to effectively deliberate.

AI Use, Limitations & Assessment

We used AI tools during two phases of the initial outlining and drafting for clarity checks. For outlining, we prompted an AI model with the game's rules and asked it to generate a hierarchical step list. The AI produced a reasonable draft but was way too verbose. With that, we edited the draft to make it more concise and relevant to the design principles outlined. We found AI most helpful for generating alternative phrasings of dense steps and for suggesting plain-language synonyms, which aligns with its strengths in editing rather than original instructional design Overall, AI served as a useful drafting assistant but not the main driver of coherent work.
